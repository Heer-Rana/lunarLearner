Types of Stars

Red Dwarfs

Red Dwarfs are the most common stars in the universe. They are small, cool, and dim compared to other stars. Red Dwarfs burn their fuel very slowly, giving them lifespans that can exceed tens of billions of years.

Mass and Size:

Red dwarfs are typically between 0.08 to 0.6 times the mass of the Sun and have a size about 10% to 50% of the Sun's diameter

Temperature:

Their surface temperature ranges from 2,500 K to 4,000 K, much cooler than the Sun's surface (~5,778 K)

Famous Red Dwarfs:

Proxima Centauri: The closest star to Earth (apart from the Sun), located about 4.24 light-years away. It has a planet in the habitable zone, called Proxima b. Barnard's Star: A nearby red dwarf star located about 6 light-years from Earth

Yellow Dwarfs

Yellow Dwarfs, like our Sun, are medium-sized stars. They emit a warm, yellowish light and have lifespans of about 10 billion years. They are located in the middle range of the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram.

Definition:

Yellow dwarfs are G-type main-sequence stars (G V stars) that produce light and heat through the fusion of hydrogen into helium in their cores. Despite their name, they often appear more white than yellow due to their high temperatures

Life Cycle

Main Sequence Phase: Yellow dwarfs spend most of their lives in the main sequence, where they stably fuse hydrogen into helium.
Red Giant Phase: Once the hydrogen fuel in the core is exhausted, the star expands and cools, becoming a red giant.
Final Stage: After shedding its outer layers, it leaves behind a white dwarf, a dense and hot stellar remnant.

Blue Giants

Blue Giants are massive, hot stars with short lifespans. They burn their fuel at a rapid pace, shining brightly but living for only a few million years before exploding as supernovae.

Formation and Life Cycle

Formation: Blue giants form from massive clouds of gas and dust. Their enormous mass triggers rapid nuclear fusion in their cores.
Main Sequence: During this phase, blue giants fuse hydrogen into helium at a tremendous rate, maintaining their bright and hot blue appearance.
End Stages: Depending on their mass, blue giants either: Explode as supernovae, leaving behind neutron stars or black holes. Shed their outer layers to form planetary nebulae, leaving behind white dwarfs less common for very massive blue giants
